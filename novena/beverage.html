Retail Food and Diabetes

How does Beverages Affect our Health?

Every day we have many opportunities to decide what to put in our mouth. But will these choices really affect the chances of getting chronic diseases like diabetes? We tried to answer this question in this experiment utilizing our weekly retail food sales dataset.

The retail sales data has 11 consumption categories (alcohol, beverages, commercially prepared items, dairy, fats and oils, fruits, grains, meats eggs and nuts, other, sugar and sweeteners, vegetables) recorded for all states, and it has volume sales with specific dates from 2019 to 2021. The numerical value is transformed to binary data (1 for high diabetes prevalence, 0 for low diabetes prevalence) using binary split based on min and max for the classification task, as well as producing the ROC curve.

The null hypothesis is that beverage sales would not affect a region’s diabetes prevalence rate, the alternative hypothesis is that beverage sales do affect states’ diabetes prevalence rate.

We chose to use the decision tree and random forest model to see the contribution of each feature. We also continued the linear regression model as comparison. At first, we generated a heatmap to see their correlatoins:

It is noticeable that the consumption of beverages is highly correlated with prevalence and has the highest correlation coefficient of 0.62. Sugar and sweeteners and fats and oils’ coefficients are also relatively high(0.6 and 0.48 respectively). The intuition is that people who have a higher consumption of beverages are more likely to develop diabetes, hence the higher diabetes prevalence rate. The heatmap result preliminarily confirms the alternative hypothesis.

Using Retail Sales Data to Predict Diabetes Prevalence -- Decision Tree Model

For the decision tree classification, we used grid search for hyperparameter tuning. We performed a grid search on the classifier using the parameters and a scorer.The parameters of the estimator used to apply these methods are optimized by cross-validated grid-search over a parameter grid. The default cross fold validation is 5 fold. The result is shown as follows:

Testing f1 score is less than training f1 score, which suggests underfitting. This is inevitable given the relatively small size of this dataset. Though F1 and recall scores are higher compared to random forest classifier, the accuracy and precision score are lower. The feature importance chart is shown as below:

Unlike the random forest model, only three features are contributing to the decision tree model, and the model is mainly affected by beverage volume sales per capita.

The model is especially weak in predicting low prevalence data where it predicts the opposite. There is a precision and recall tradeoff in the model, as reducing the threshold, precision decreases and recall increases.
We combined two model’s ROC curves in the following figure:

For ROC curves, the ROC curve shows the trade-off between sensitivity (or TPR) and specificity (1 – FPR). Classifiers that give curves closer to the top-left corner indicate better performance. From the ROC curve of both models, both show the tendency towards the top-left corner, which means both models perform well. The decision tree model’s ROC curve is slightly closer to the top-left corner, but not too much, hence we could only say that the decision tree model is slightly superior to the random forest classifier.

Test the Importance of different categories on diabetes --- Linear Regression

We also tested this dataset using linear regression. The result is summarized as follows:

The p-value of beverage sales per capita is very small, which suggests that it is statistically significant in providing information in predicting the region’s diabetes prevalence rate. The R-squared value is 0.979, which means the data is well fitted to the regression line, and 97.9% variability in prevalence can be explained by retail sales. We hence conclude that beverage retails sales can significantly affect the region’s diabetes prevalence rate. We therefore can reject the null hypothesis.

The result suggests that one particular component of diet we should pay attention to is beverages. Sweetened sodas and lattes don't generate the same feelings of satiety as solid foods do, and most of the extra calories come from sugar and fat.
