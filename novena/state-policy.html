State Policy and Diabetes

The Influence of Diabetes Rate on State Policy


If we take a look at the diabetes prevalence rate across the US, it is noticeable that certain southern states, particularly Mississippi, West Virginia, Kentucky, Arkansas, and Alabama have higher prevalence rate above the national average.

The following figure contains the distribution of the total health-related policy from 2011 to 2017 and their statuses. The total policy number varies largely among states, we have state like New York that have over 3200 policies at the top and state like South Dakota which only have 53 policies in 6 years. For the majority of states, the dead rate surpasses 50% which indicates passing health-related policies faces certain obstacles.


The previous figure highlights some states that have the highest prevalence rate, but it seems like even for thoses states, the total policies count is not very high. Take West Virginia as an example, though its prevalence rate is very high (15.2%), its policy count ranks in the middle. The following map indicates the enacted policy count map, the southern part has a higher amount of policies passed than the middle west region, but it is not as high as the northeast region. These states should pay more attention to the diabetes prevention legislation.


If we look at the breakdown of health-related policies, we see a surge started in 2008 and the policy number decreases dramatically in 2012. Other background information is needed for a more detailed explanation, one assumption is that the health funding has been increased in this period.

We also looked at California's enacted policy rate and diabetes prevalence data. R-squared value is 0.656, which indicates 65.6% variability of diabetes prevalence can be explained by the enacted policy rate. However we only have 5-year data, the conclusion is not certain, but it does suggest that to some extent, states like California tend to pass more health-related policies as the diabetes prevalence rate increases.
