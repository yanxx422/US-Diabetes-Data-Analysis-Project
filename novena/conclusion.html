Conclusions

Conclusions

Through our in-depth analysis and experiments, we found that Diabetes data varies among different demographic groups. People of different ages, genders and races can have different probabilities of developing diabetes. Even among people diagnosed with diabetes, the influence on their daily life(amputation and depression) can be different. In the United States, lifestyle contributors can be especially profound among minority groups. In addition to Native Americans (including Alaskan and Hawaiian Natives), Blacks and Hispanics not only have a higher incidence of diabetes, but also greater challenges in controlling the disease, and therefore are more likely to experience complications. Although the usual risk factors are at play, an inherited genetic tendency, plus a poor diet, sedentary lifestyle, and obesity—societal problems clearly magnify those risks among large swaths of the population, particularly among those who are economically disadvantaged.

Counterintuitively, we found that eating vegetables doesn't help reduce the rate of diabetes. Though further experiments need to be done to confirm, our limited data suggests that beverage consumption plays a more important rule, as our regression analysis and machine learning model indicates that beverages sales are highly correlated with diabetes prevalence rate.

The largest risk factor leading to diabetes is obesity. For some southern states like Mississippi and Alabama, people tend to be more sedentary and have the highest diabetes prevalence rate. But the health related policy count for those states is not at the top. More actions need to be taken in these states at legislation level for better diabetes prevention.

The relationship between prevalence rate and enacted policies is not correlative. However, in certain state like California, there is strong relation between prevalence rate and enacted policies, which may bring a possible conclusion that if the diabetes prevalence rate goes high, then states is more likely a pass health-related policy. However, we only have five year data, and we need further investigations and analyses on specific states’ cases.

Limitations and Improvements

Our experiments only included group datasets, lack of personal records. Most conclusions come from crude prevalence data. Survey data is inaccurate as there are problems with overestimation and underestimation based on personal perception.

Data size is too small for machine learning models because We had to transform categorical data to numerical data. After combining data process and data cleaning, the remaining data size is very limited.

We assume strong correlation between obesity and diabetes which can be a problem.(only get obesity rate with behavior data together/ lack of direct correlation analysis between behavior data and diabetes prevalence)

We also need to consider other background information and time factors when analyzing legislation-related data. For example, policy numbers are very high from 2008 to 2012, was there a growth in health care spending?
