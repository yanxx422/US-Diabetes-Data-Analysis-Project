Inequalities and Diabetes

Inequalities and Obesity

Obesity is believed to account for 80-85% of the risk of developing type 2 diabetes, while recent research suggests that obese people are up to 80 times more likely to develop type 2 diabetes than those with BMI of less than 22.

Obesity is an important factor that could potentially lead to diabetes. The following figure illustrate obesity rate among different income groups. First thing to notice is that all groupsâ€™ obesity prevalence rate are increasing. People with income less than $15000 has the highest obesity rate, and people with higher income are generally have lower obesity rate. People with income less than $15000 has an obesity rate of 37.1% in 2019, which is higher than people with income over $75000 (29.67%).

We divided the data into 3 income level groups and conducted t-Test between groups. All the p-Value(5.89e-153) is much lower than 0.05 or 0.01 which proved our alternative hypothesis: There is an association between obesity rates and income.

If we look at the obesity rate breakdown among different racial groups. Hawaiian/Pacific Islander(38.45%) and Non-Hispanic black adults(37.76%) has much higher obesity rate than white adults (28.06%). Asians has the lowest obesity rate, which is only 10.45%, almost one-fourth of that for Hawaiian/Pacific Islanders.

Prevalence and Further Influence of Diabetes in Different Demographic Groups

Diabetes is increasing in the U.S., but varies widely by ethnic group. Non-Hispanic Black Adults has the highest prevalence of diabetes (14.248%) overall which is twice higher than White Adults. Males has higher prevalence of diabetes than females but not significant.

Even among patients with diagnosed diabetes, the impact of diabetes on their lives varies greatly according to their demographic groups. Diabetes is responsible for more than 60% of lower limb amputations not caused by accidental injuries. American Indians has the highest amputation rate (8.879 %) overall, followed by non-Hispanic black adults (5.665%). Males has much higher rate of amputation than females. American Indians and Alaska natives has the highest mortality rate per 100,000 deaths, 129.8, followed by non-hispanic black adults with 102.23.

People with diabetes are two to three times more likely to have depression according to CDC Report. Despite having the highest prevalence of diabetes, Black adult and hispanic adult patients have the lowest depression rate, 24.78% and 29.02 % respectively. Female patients have higher risk (35.72%) of developing depression compared with males (22.95%). Depression may result from poorly understood biochemical changes caused by diabetes itself or may develop from the stress of living with a chronic disease.
